Frequently Asked Questions

  • What are the current gun control laws in the United States?

    • Some of the gun control laws have been... The National Firearms Act (1934) regulates the sale and transfer of certain firearms, including machine guns and short-barreled rifles, to enhance public safety by imposing strict regulations and background checks. The Gun Control Act (1968) aims to prevent firearm sales to individuals deemed dangerous or irresponsible by establishing prohibited categories and regulating interstate commerce in firearms. The Brady Handgun Violence Prevention Act (1993) mandates federal background checks on firearm purchasers to prevent sales to prohibited individuals, enhancing the effectiveness of gun control measures.

  • How do background checks work for purchasing firearms?

    • Federal law requires federally licensed firearms dealers (but not private sellers) to initiate a background check on the purchaser prior to sale of a firearm. Federal law provides states with the option of serving as a state “point of contact” and conducting their own background checks using state, as well as federal, records and databases, or having the checks performed by the FBI using only the federal National Instant Criminal Background Check System (“NICS”) database. (Note that state files are not always included in the federal database.)

Dispelling Myths

Let's address some common myths about firearms:

  • Myth: More guns lead to less crime.

    • According to Research Gate, in an article by Gary Kleck and E. Britt Patterson,their assertion suggests, "Findings indicate that (1) gun prevalence levels generally have no net positive effect on total violence rates, (2) homicide, gun assault, and rape rates increase gun prevalence, (3) gun control restrictions have no net effect on gun prevalence levels, and (4) most gun control restrictions generally have no net effect on violence rates. There were, however, some possible exceptions to this last conclusion—of 108 assessments of effects of different gun laws on different types of violence, 7 indicated good support, and another 11 partial support, for the hypothesis of gun control efficacy." In conclusion, some people may say guns actually lead to more crime, and some may say it doesn't change. We believe safer guns = safer people.
